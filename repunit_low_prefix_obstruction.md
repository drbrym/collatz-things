# A Low-Prefix Obstruction to Fixed Block Floors

**Building on:** `repunit_256_block_target.md`,
`repunit_tail_merge_reduction.md`
**Status:** Proved compatibility and non-descent results. This note does not
construct an active counterexample to the 256-Block Floor because an
off-diagonal merger has not been excluded.

## 1. The adversarial prefix

For each \(K\ge1\), consider the valuation word

\[
\mathbf e_K=(2,\underbrace{1,\ldots,1}_{K-1}),
\]

whose total valuation is

\[
E_K=K+1.
\]

This is far below the proposed 256-block floor when \(K=256\):

\[
E_{256}=257<425.
\]

The word is not merely an abstract Collatz pattern. It occurs on an explicit
infinite residue class of odd repunit exponents.

---

## 2. Every finite low prefix occurs on the repunit curve

For a valuation word \(\mathbf e=(e_0,\ldots,e_{K-1})\), let

\[
E=\sum_{i<K}e_i,
\qquad
E_i=\sum_{j<i}e_j,
\]

and

\[
c(\mathbf e)
=\sum_{i=0}^{K-1}3^{K-1-i}2^{E_i}.
\]

The word is realised exactly by one odd residue class \(x\bmod2^{E+1}\):

\[
\boxed{
x\equiv
\bigl(2^E-c(\mathbf e)\bigr)3^{-K}
\pmod{2^{E+1}}.
}
\]

Indeed, the accumulated numerator must be divisible by \(2^E\) but not by
\(2^{E+1}\):

\[
3^Kx+c(\mathbf e)\equiv2^E\pmod{2^{E+1}}.
\]

**Theorem 1.** For every \(K\ge1\), there is a unique odd residue class

\[
n\equiv n_K\pmod{2^{K+1}}
\]

such that the repunit \(a_n=(3^n-1)/2\) has initial valuation word
\(\mathbf e_K\).

**Proof.** Let \(r_K\) be the exact odd starting-value class modulo
\(2^{K+2}\) associated with \(\mathbf e_K\), since \(E=K+1\). The repunit
condition is

\[
a_n\equiv r_K\pmod{2^{K+2}},
\]

equivalently

\[
3^n\equiv2r_K+1\pmod{2^{K+3}}.
\]

Because the first valuation is exactly \(2\), \(r_K\equiv1\pmod8\), so
\(2r_K+1\equiv3\pmod{16}\), in particular \(3\pmod8\).

For \(M\ge3\), the cyclic subgroup generated by \(3\) modulo \(2^M\) has
order \(2^{M-2}\) and consists exactly of the odd units congruent to \(1\) or
\(3\pmod8\). Its odd powers are exactly those congruent to \(3\pmod8\).
Therefore the displayed congruence has one odd solution modulo
\(2^{M-2}=2^{K+1}\). Every exponent in that class realises the valuation word.
\(\blacksquare\)

Thus finite valuation compatibility cannot establish a block floor larger than
\(K+1\).

## 3. The low prefix does not descend

**Theorem 2.** Let \(n\ge3\) be an odd exponent realising
\(\mathbf e_K\). Then

\[
x_t(n)>2^n-1
\qquad(0\le t\le K).
\]

**Proof.** At time \(t\ge1\), the accumulated valuation is \(E_t=t+1\).
The affine accumulation has positive correction, so

\[
x_t(n)
>
\frac{3^t a_n}{2^{t+1}}
=\frac12\left(\frac32\right)^t a_n.
\]

Also

\[
\frac{a_n}{2^n-1}
=\frac{3^n-1}{2(2^n-1)}
>\frac43
\]

for odd \(n\ge3\). Hence, for \(t\ge1\),

\[
\frac{x_t(n)}{2^n-1}
>
\frac12\left(\frac32\right)^t\frac43
\ge1.
\]

The case \(t=0\) is immediate from \(a_n>2^n-1\). \(\blacksquare\)

So any mechanism saving the 256-Block Floor on this exponent class must be a
merger, not prior descent.

## 4. Equal-diagonal-state collision is impossible

**Theorem 3.** A tail realising \(\mathbf e_K\) cannot, during its first
\(K\) steps, share the same full diagonal state \((d,E,A)\) with a smaller odd
exponent.

**Proof.** At time \(t\ge1\), the low-prefix tail has cumulative valuation

\[
E_t=t+1.
\]

Suppose it merged on the same diagonal with a smaller odd exponent \(m<n\) at
source time \(j\). Equality of diagonal coordinates gives

\[
n+t=m+j,
\qquad
j-t=n-m.
\]

Since \(m,n\) are odd and \(m<n\), \(n-m\ge2\). Thus \(j\ge t+2\).

Every \(j\)-step shortcut trajectory has cumulative valuation at least \(j\).
But equality of full diagonal states would require the source cumulative valuation
to equal \(E_t=t+1<j\), a contradiction. \(\blacksquare\)

Therefore a merger preserving the proposed 256-floor cannot use the
equal-\((d,E,A)\) mechanism established in `repunit_tail_merge_reduction.md`.
It would have to be either:

1. an off-diagonal merger; or
2. an exact same-coordinate equality arising from distinct normal-form pairs
   \((E,A)\), a mechanism not observed in the finite merger data.

## 5. Explicit 256-step residue

For \(K=256\), the least positive odd representative produced by the exact
discrete logarithm is

\[
\begin{aligned}
n_{256}={}&
45668407499936535198320256527201491097067812606246254032465685587732141442221.
\end{aligned}
\]

Modulo \(2^{257}\), every exponent congruent to \(n_{256}\) has first 256
valuations

\[
(2,1^{255})
\]

and total valuation \(257\).

The representative has 255 binary digits. It is generated by a short exact
algorithm from the integer \(256\); its large modulus does not imply high
Kolmogorov complexity.

## 6. Closed form and the 2-adic enemy branch

During the word \(\mathbf e_K\), the first step divides by \(4\), and every
later step divides by \(2\). The recurrence \(y\mapsto(3y+1)/2\) gives

\[
x_K+1
=\frac{3^{K-1}(3^{n+1}+7)}{2^{K+2}}.
\]

Consequently:

**Theorem 4.** An odd exponent \(n\) realises \(\mathbf e_K\) if and only if

\[
v_2(3^{n+1}+7)\ge K+3.
\]

**Proof.** The displayed closed form shows that the first \(K\) shortcut
divisions are \(2,1,\ldots,1\) exactly when \(x_K\) is odd, equivalently when
the numerator \(3^{n+1}+7\) is divisible by \(2^{K+3}\). \(\blacksquare\)

The residue classes are nested:

\[
n_{K+1}\equiv n_K\pmod{2^{K+1}}.
\]

They converge in \(\mathbb Z_2\) to the unique odd 2-adic exponent
\(\alpha\) satisfying

\[
3^{\alpha+1}=-7.
\]

More precisely, writing \(\beta=(\alpha+1)/2\), the equation is

\[
9^\beta=-7.
\]

Since \(9\) topologically generates \(1+8\mathbb Z_2\) and
\(-7\in1+8\mathbb Z_2\), this equation has a unique solution
\(\beta\in\mathbb Z_2\).

There is no claim that \(\alpha\) is a positive ordinary integer. Its finite
truncations are ordinary exponents producing arbitrarily long low-valuation
prefixes.

## 7. No finite state-free recovery bound

**Corollary 5.** Fix \(K\) and any finite horizon \(H\). There is an odd
positive exponent whose first \(K+H\) valuations are

\[
(2,1^{K+H-1}).
\]

In particular, after the first \(K\) steps its local valuation deficit cannot
recover during the next \(H\) steps.

**Proof.** Choose any positive representative of the class
\(n_{K+H}\pmod{2^{K+H+1}}\) and apply Theorem 1. Every added valuation equals
\(1<\log_2 3\), so the local surplus continues to decrease. \(\blacksquare\)

Thus no theorem of the form

> “a deficit determined only by a finite valuation block must recover within a
> fixed function of that block”

can hold without using additional global state or a merger mechanism.

## 8. Ordinary-size bound on the enemy branch

The 2-adic branch gives arbitrarily long finite prefixes as \(n\) varies, but
an individual positive exponent cannot shadow it for arbitrarily long relative
to its ordinary size.

**Theorem 6.** If a positive odd exponent \(n\ge3\) realises
\(\mathbf e_K\), then

\[
\boxed{
K<\log_2(3)(n+1)-2.
}
\]

In particular,

\[
\frac Kn<\log_2 3+O(1/n),
\]

so this branch cannot survive for \(3n\) steps.

**Proof.** Theorem 4 gives

\[
2^{K+3}\le3^{n+1}+7.
\]

For \(n\ge3\), \(7<3^{n+1}\), hence

\[
3^{n+1}+7<2\cdot3^{n+1}.
\]

Taking base-\(2\) logarithms yields

\[
K+3<1+(n+1)\log_2 3,
\]

which is the stated inequality. \(\blacksquare\)

This is a model non-shadowing argument: combine a 2-adic divisibility
condition with an ordinary Archimedean size bound.

## 9. Consequences for the proof programme

The proposed 256-Block Floor can only hold if:

> Every sufficiently large exponent
> \(n\equiv n_{256}\pmod{2^{257}}\) reaches a smaller pre-descent repunit tail
> before step 256 through a merger mechanism other than equality of full
> diagonal states.

This is much stronger than the merger behaviour seen in the finite data:
all \(4783\) exact mergers through \(n\le10001\) have equal full diagonal
states.

Accordingly:

1. local valuation compatibility cannot prove the floor;
2. equal-diagonal-state collision theory cannot prove the floor;
3. a proof must either establish a new off-diagonal merger theorem for these
   explicit classes or replace the fixed block-floor target.

The variable-window recovery proposal is also insufficient if its bound
depends only on the observed finite block: Corollary 5 extends the low run past
any such finite horizon.

The remaining viable forms are global:

1. prove a new merger theorem beyond equal full diagonal states for the nested
   enemy classes; or
2. prove a long-range non-shadowing theorem showing that a positive integer
   exponent cannot follow any low-valuation 2-adic branch for a window linear
   in the exponent itself.

---

Theorem 6 shows that the explicit enemy branch does not threaten the empirical
\(3n\) window. What it defeats is a fixed local block floor. The global route
should seek analogous size bounds for more general low-valuation branches.

---

## 10. Entropy-track warning

The same construction blocks a naive Kolmogorov-complexity argument.
A residue modulo an enormous power of two can still have a very short
description: “the discrete-log residue associated with
\((2,1^{K-1})\).”

Thus low description complexity does not prevent an exponent from selecting a
high-modulus survivor class. Any information-theoretic proof needs a concrete
non-shadowing or discrepancy theorem, not only a comparison between modulus
size and the bit-length of \(n\).
