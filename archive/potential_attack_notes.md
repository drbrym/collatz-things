# Potential-Function Attack Notes

**Status:** exploratory; not a proof

This note records a first attempt to combine the Rail-7 closed form with the
Block-Fracture fuel idea.

---

## 1. The candidate fuel

For odd `x`, define the trailing-one fuel

$$
\tau(x) = v_2(x+1).
$$

On Rail 7, write `x = 8y+7`. Then

$$
\tau(x)=v_2(x+1)=v_2(8(y+1))=v_2(y+1)+3.
$$

The Rail-7 survival depth is therefore

$$
D(y)=\left\lfloor \frac{v_2(y+1)}{2}\right\rfloor
     =\left\lfloor \frac{\tau(x)-3}{2}\right\rfloor.
$$

So Rail-7 growth is powered directly by trailing-one fuel. In binary terms,
hard Rail-7 cases are near-Mersenne numbers:

$$
x = 2^L m - 1.
$$

This is exactly the regime where the Block-Fracture Lemma becomes relevant:
long trailing blocks of ones are not stable under multiplication by `3`.

---

## 2. First simple potential

Try

$$
P_c(x)=\log_2 x + c\,\tau(x).
$$

The sign treats a long trailing-one block as stored danger: if a Collatz macro
burns fuel and lowers value, `P_c` should fall.

Computational scan in `explore_potential.py`:

- Across sampled Rail-7 escape macros, the potential wants roughly

  $$
  c \gtrsim 0.64.
  $$

- Across ordinary first-descent epochs, this fails immediately on recharge
  events such as

  $$
  169 \mapsto 127.
  $$

  Here the value decreases, but

  $$
  \tau(169)=1,\qquad \tau(127)=7.
  $$

  Thus the same potential that is strong enough to price Rail-7 fuel punishes
  recharge events too heavily.

Conclusion: a one-term fuel potential cannot work globally.

---

## 3. What the failure says

The obstruction is not Rail 7 itself. Rail 7 is controlled: fuel is consumed at
an exact rate. The obstruction is **recharge**:

$$
\text{low fuel} \longrightarrow \text{high fuel}
$$

can occur while the raw value descends.

So a viable proof cannot merely assign a fixed penalty to current trailing-one
fuel. It must distinguish:

1. fuel being consumed during a Rail-7 burst, from
2. fuel being created by a rare congruence event.

That points toward a phase-aware potential or a recharge-counting lemma.

---

## 4. Proof-shaped target

The desired theorem is not "long runs always shrink"; Block-Fracture explicitly
rules out that overclaim. The target is narrower:

> Long Rail-7 survival requires a long trailing block of ones, and after that
> fuel is consumed, recreating comparable fuel requires a specific low-bit
> congruence whose cost cannot be paid indefinitely.

In practical terms, the next lemma to seek is:

**Recharge Cost Lemma.** To move from small `tau` to `tau >= L`, the trajectory
must pass through a residue window of size `2^-L`, and the surrounding affine
macro has enough division to compensate for the future Rail-7 burst.

The Block-Fracture Lemma helps here as an anti-renewal statement: long blocks
of ones do not self-preserve under `3x`; they must be regenerated by separate
low-bit alignment.

---

## 5. Next experiment

Replace the simple potential with a phase-aware one:

$$
P(x)=\log_2 x + c\,\tau(x) - R(x),
$$

where `R(x)` credits only those high-fuel states that were just produced by a
verified recharge macro. Equivalently, work with paired macros:

$$
\text{recharge} \rightarrow \text{Rail-7 burn} \rightarrow \text{escape}.
$$

The proof question becomes:

> Does every recharge-burn pair have net multiplicative drift below `1`,
> unless it merges into a smaller certified class?
